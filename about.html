ABOUT THIS PROJECT

A final-year investigation into the future of a dam that feeds two cities.

Rawal Dam supplies drinking water to Islamabad and Rawalpindi. It was commissioned in 1962 with a design capacity of 58.59 MCM. A combination of sediment loading, urbanisation, and climate stress has reduced its live storage over six decades. This Decision Support System was built to quantify that trajectory, project its end-of-life under business-as-usual conditions, and evaluate the policy options that could defer reservoir failure.

Honest Limitations & Future Work

Every model has boundaries. Naming ours is part of doing the work honestly.

Known limitations

  • Landsat 30 m resolution undercounts village-scale and farmhouse-level development in peri-urban zones.
  • Hargreaves–Samani is a fallback evapotranspiration model used because the PMD record does not consistently include radiation and wind data required by the full Penman–Monteith method.
  • The 2088 baseline end-of-life is a point estimate. A probabilistic envelope with climate ensemble inputs would strengthen the forecast confidence bounds.
  • The CA-Markov allocator does not model urban demolition or contraction — once a pixel is allocated to urban it remains urban for the simulation horizon.
  • Cost scores in the MCDA are grounded in MES Rawalpindi 2-2025 rates for the intervention-specific cost estimator; policy complexity scores are literature-based relative estimates and are flagged as such on the Scenarios page.

Natural extensions

  • Integrate Sentinel-2 10 m imagery to capture village-scale sprawl the Landsat grid misses along Murree Road and Simly Road.
  • Replace the single climate multiplier with CMIP6 regional climate model ensembles to produce storage-decline confidence intervals.
  • Add a sensitivity-analysis module quantifying how EOL year shifts with each calibration constant (r, A, base sediment rate, trap efficiency).
  • Build a fiscal-year phased-implementation module so the cost estimator can report capital outlay distributed across a realistic construction schedule rather than lump-sum.
  • Connect the DSS to real-time PMD and SDO feeds so baseline forecasts continuously re-anchor as new observations arrive.
