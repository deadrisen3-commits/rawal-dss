TECHNICAL METHODOLOGY

How the Decision Support System computes everything it shows

This page documents the eight-engine Python pipeline that underpins every number, map, and forecast on this site. The pipeline runs end-to-end from raw Landsat imagery and meteorological station data to a calibrated CA-Markov land-use forecaster and five policy-scenario projections. Every equation, threshold, and calibration constant is listed so the methodology is reproducible and defensible.

Overview

The Rawal Dam Decision Support System answers one core question: given observed hydrological, climatic, and land-use trends, when does the reservoir cross its dead-storage threshold, and how much of that collapse can be deferred by each candidate policy?

The pipeline is structured as eight sequential engines. Each engine consumes the outputs of its predecessors, so the whole workflow runs top-to-bottom in a single Jupyter notebook and produces (a) a 10-page executive PDF report, (b) a structured JSON file that powers every page of this website, and (c) a folder of georeferenced composite overlays for the interactive map.

Engine 1 · Lake Surface Area Extraction

Purpose: measure how much of Rawal Lake's open-water surface area has been lost per year, 2000–2024, as a proxy for effective storage loss to sedimentation.

Method

For each year in the analysis window, the script pulls the cloud-minimised Landsat Surface Reflectance composite, crops to the lake polygon, and computes the Normalised Difference Water Index:

$$ \text{NDWI} = \frac{\rho_{\text{Green}} - \rho_{\text{NIR}}}{\rho_{\text{Green}} + \rho_{\text{NIR}}} $$

A pixel is classified as open water when NDWI exceeds a per-year adaptive threshold calibrated from the distribution's upper tail (typically in the range 0.1–0.3 depending on turbidity and atmospheric conditions). Classified pixels are counted and multiplied by the Landsat pixel footprint (30 m × 30 m = 900 m²) to yield surface area in square metres, then converted to acres.

Output

historical.water_area_acres[] — one value per year, written to rawal_data.json. This array powers the Dashboard's Hydrology tab and anchors the volumetric calculation in Engine 4.

Engine 2 · Evaporation (Hargreaves–Samani)

Purpose: estimate annual evaporative loss from the lake surface using only temperature data (which the PMD station provides reliably) rather than Penman-Monteith (which needs radiation and wind data we don't have on a consistent basis).

Method

Daily reference evapotranspiration is computed via the Hargreaves-Samani formula:

$$ ET_o = 0.0023 \cdot R_a \cdot (T_{\text{mean}} + 17.8) \cdot \sqrt{T_{\max} - T_{\min}} $$

where $R_a$ is extraterrestrial radiation (computed from latitude and Julian day), and temperatures are in °C. $ET_o$ is then scaled to open-water evaporation $E_{\text{lake}}$ by a pan coefficient of approximately 1.05 (open water evaporates slightly faster than reference grass).

Daily values are summed to an annual total, then multiplied by the year's lake surface area (from Engine 1) to convert depth (mm) to volume (MCM).

Why Hargreaves-Samani rather than Penman-Monteith

Penman-Monteith is the FAO-56 gold standard but requires net radiation, wind speed, and humidity at the station. PMD's Islamabad and Rawalpindi stations do not publish all of these consistently across the 25-year window. Hargreaves-Samani requires only $T_{\min}$ and $T_{\max}$, which are available continuously, and it is accepted by FAO-56 as a fallback when complete data is unavailable.

Engine 3 · Urban Sprawl Detection (NDBI)

Purpose: identify which pixels inside the watershed converted from non-urban to urban between 2000 and the analysis year.

Method

The Normalised Difference Built-up Index is computed per year from Landsat SWIR and NIR bands:

$$ \text{NDBI} = \frac{\rho_{\text{SWIR}} - \rho_{\text{NIR}}}{\rho_{\text{SWIR}} + \rho_{\text{NIR}}} $$

NDBI is normalised per year to the 2nd–98th percentile range to produce a bounded [0, 1] signal that is robust to atmospheric differences between acquisition dates. Two thresholds are then applied:

  • Core urban (2000 baseline): pixels with normalised NDBI > 0.85 are tagged as already-built in the year 2000. This establishes the pre-analysis urban footprint.
  • New sprawl (per year): pixels where the change in normalised NDBI between 2000 and the analysis year exceeds $\Delta\text{NDBI} > 0.30$ are classified as newly-urbanised.

The cumulative sprawl mask is the union of all per-year new-sprawl masks. Acreage is computed as pixel count × 900 m² ÷ 4046.86.

Why two thresholds

A single absolute NDBI cut would over-count bare rock and dry ridges in the upper Margallas (which have NDBI signatures similar to concrete). Requiring a change of ≥ 0.30 above a pixel's 2000 baseline ensures the pipeline only counts genuine transitions, not static bare surfaces.

Calibration against ground truth

The 2024 absolute urban total (17,599 acres, or 26.3 % of the 67,021-acre watershed) was cross-checked against published Rawal catchment LULC studies (2012 baseline of ~15 % built-up) and found consistent with extrapolated 2024 urbanisation for the Islamabad fringe.

Engine 4 · Geometric Storage Decline

Purpose: convert observed surface-area loss (Engine 1) plus urbanisation (Engine 3) into a physically-defensible storage-decline rate, then calibrate a forward projector.

The core relationship

Two independent anchor points define the storage-decline line:

  • ISRIP 2000 measured volume: 46.12 MCM (field survey of record)
  • Original design capacity: 58.59 MCM (commissioning, 1962)
  • Dead storage threshold: 5.30 MCM (end-of-life condition)

Annual storage is reconstructed for each year in the analysis window by applying an unforced geometric relationship between surface-area loss and volume loss, calibrated so the 2000 and 2024 endpoints reproduce observed values.

The sediment multiplier

The base sediment loading rate is modulated by a sprawl multiplier that captures the physical reality that more built-up watershed surface means more runoff, more erosion, more sediment transport:

$$ \Delta V_{\text{year}} = r_{\text{base}} \cdot (1 + f_{\text{transformed}}) \cdot m_{\text{climate}} $$

where:

  • $r_{\text{base}} = 0.3282$ MCM/yr — base sediment rate calibrated against 2000–2024 observed decline
  • $f_{\text{transformed}} = \dfrac{\text{new sprawl acres since 2000}}{\text{total watershed acres}}$
  • $m_{\text{climate}} = 1.05$ — climate multiplier accounting for +5 % precipitation trend in regional projections

Under Scenario A (Business-As-Usual) this equation run forward year-by-year from 2024 until storage crosses the 5.3 MCM dead-storage threshold, which the pipeline computes as the year 2088. That is the baseline end-of-life used throughout the DSS.

Engine 5 · CA-Markov Land-Use Forecaster

Purpose: project where future urban growth will fall spatially, not just how much.

The logistic demand curve

Aggregate urban demand (in acres) is governed by a three-parameter logistic fit to the 2000–2024 observed sprawl series:

$$ U(t) = \frac{K}{1 + A \cdot e^{-r \cdot t}} $$

where $t$ is years since 2000 and:

  • $K = 35{,}000$ acres — carrying capacity (topographical sprawl ceiling, pixels with slope ≤ 15°)
  • $A = 5.3484$ — shape parameter from nonlinear least-squares fit
  • $r = 0.07034$ — growth rate from the same fit

The spatial allocation — Moore neighbourhood with slope weighting

Each year's new urban demand is allocated pixel-by-pixel using a Cellular Automata rule with a Moore neighbourhood (8 nearest neighbours):

  1. Compute a transition-potential map: for every non-urban pixel, count how many of its 8 neighbours are already urban (the Moore kernel).
  2. Weight the potential by inverse slope (steeper = less likely to urbanise).
  3. Apply a Kappa-boost factor that rewards pixels near the current urban frontier, accelerating realistic edge-expansion.
  4. Rank all candidate pixels and allocate the year's demand to the top-ranked N pixels (where N is the acres demanded ÷ 0.222 acres/pixel).

Why this beats naive logistic

A pure demand model tells you total sprawl but not where it goes. A pure CA model over-clusters. The combination — demand driven by logistic, spatial allocation driven by slope-weighted neighbourhood — reproduces the real observed pattern of sprawl advancing valley-by-valley along road corridors while avoiding steep slopes.

Engine 6 · Mitigation Scenarios

Purpose: quantify the end-of-life (EOL) year and volume preserved under each of five candidate policy pathways.

ID Name Intervention EOL year Years gained
A Business As Usual No action. Full unmitigated trajectory. 2088 0
B Check Dam Cascades 25 % sediment trap efficiency via engineered check dams across tributaries. 2107 +19
C Strict Zoning 3 km lake buffer + 7° slope cap on new development. 2091 +3
D Biological Afforestation 300 acres/year of watershed reforestation. 2096 +8
E Ultimate Hybrid Policy All three measures combined. 2130 +42

How each intervention modifies the sediment equation

  • Check dams (B, E) — multiply annual $\Delta V$ by $(1 - \eta_{\text{trap}})$ where $\eta_{\text{trap}} = 0.25$.
  • Zoning (C, E) — in the CA-Markov allocator, pixels inside the buffer or above slope cap are excluded from the candidate pool, reducing realised sprawl relative to logistic demand.
  • Afforestation (D, E) — reclaim acres accumulate annually and subtract from the effective transformed fraction $f_{\text{transformed}}$ in the sprawl multiplier.

An honest note about afforestation

In this pipeline, afforestation reclaims previously non-urban degraded land — it does not demolish existing concrete. This is the realistic assumption for policy modelling in a live urbanising watershed. The Scenario D mask therefore shows new forest cover filling in bare ground, not replacing Bani Gala or Bharakahu.

Engine 7 · Executive PDF Report

Purpose: produce a 10-page decision-ready document summarising every output above.

Engine 7 consumes the in-memory outputs of Engines 1–6 and renders them using matplotlib with a custom colour palette (forest #c8e6c9, urban #555555, lake #1e88e5, reclaimed #76ff03). Each scenario page applies a peri-urban correction — the already-built 2024 footprint is dilated by 4 pixels along the lake buffer before rendering, so maps never falsely show a lakeshore stripped of its real concrete.

The output is RAWAL_FINAL_MASTER_REPORT.pdf, available on the Downloads page.

Engine 8 · Web Export

Purpose: render every pipeline output in formats this website can consume.

  • data/core/rawal_data.json — the full numeric record: metadata, historical arrays, watershed totals, logistic coefficients, forecast baseline, and the five scenario trajectories.
  • data/overlays/*.png — composite raster overlays matching the PDF's visual identity. Each pixel outside the watershed polygon is fully transparent so the maps sit cleanly over the Leaflet basemap. Bounds are reprojected from UTM to WGS84 by reprojecting the four corners individually, avoiding the safety-padding inflation that rasterio.transform_bounds adds.
  • data/geo/*.geojson — watershed and lake vector boundaries.
  • data/manifest.json — an index of every exported asset for the website to query.

2024 display reconciliation

The cumulative sprawl mask aggregated inside Engine 3 can exhibit shape-alignment gaps where year-to-year Landsat crop rectangles diverge by a few pixels. This does not affect the CA-Markov scenario outputs (which rebuild their own grid) but can understate the 2024 visual map. Engine 8 therefore re-derives the 2024 display mask directly from the 2024 Landsat NDBI at a 0.80 threshold, unioned with the Phase-3 baseline, producing a clean display value. The numeric thesis value (17,599 acres, 26.3 %) inside rawal_data.json is unchanged.

Validation

Every scenario output was validated against two independent checks:

  1. Numerical validation — the interactive Simulator on this site implements the same sediment-accounting equations in JavaScript. Reproducing each scenario's slider settings recovers its EOL year within ±2 years (±5 for the hybrid), confirming the math engine is not specific to the Python implementation.
  2. Spatial validation — the 2000 and 2024 urban footprints were visually compared against Google Earth historical imagery and published Rawal LULC studies (2012 baseline of ~15 % built-up). The Margalla ridge correctly shows minimal urbanisation; the Bani Gala–Bharakahu–Murree Road corridor correctly shows heavy development.

Limitations & honest caveats

  • Landsat 30 m resolution undercounts village-scale development. Adding Sentinel-2 10 m data would pick up roughly 15–25 % more peri-urban pixels in the Murree Road and Simly Road corridors.
  • Hargreaves-Samani is a fallback ET model. Where full Penman-Monteith data exist, that should be preferred.
  • The 2088 baseline EOL is a point estimate, not a confidence interval. A Monte Carlo envelope would be a natural extension.
  • Cost and complexity scores in the MCDA — longevity and volume metrics are pipeline-derived, but cost and complexity are literature-based relative estimates. The Cost Estimator module on the Scenarios page provides a bottom-up alternative using MES Rawalpindi 2-2025 rates.
  • CA-Markov does not model demolition — urban pixels, once allocated, remain urban for the simulation horizon. Afforestation only reclaims non-urban degraded land.
